Does OHIP Cover You Outside Canada? What Ontarians Need to Know (2026)

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

Short answer: no — OHIP provides virtually no useful coverage once you leave Canada. Since Ontario ended its Out-of-Country Travellers Program in 2020, OHIP does not reimburse emergency hospital or doctor bills abroad. If you travel outside the country without travel medical insurance, you are personally responsible for the entire cost of any emergency.

This is one of the most common — and most expensive — misunderstandings among Ontario travellers. Below is exactly what OHIP does and doesn't cover when you leave the province and the country, and how to close the gap before your next trip.

OHIP outside Ontario, but inside Canada

If you travel to another Canadian province or territory, you're in reasonable shape. Through interprovincial billing agreements, OHIP covers medically necessary physician and hospital services almost everywhere in Canada (Quebec is a partial exception for some physician services, where you may need to pay and claim reimbursement).

What OHIP still does not cover elsewhere in Canada:

  • Ambulance fees — these are billed by the province you're in.
  • Prescription drugs outside a hospital.
  • Dental, vision, and paramedical care.

For a cross-Canada road trip, a basic travel or health plan still adds valuable protection — but the bigger risk is international travel.

OHIP outside Canada: the hard truth

Before 2020, OHIP offered a small out-of-country reimbursement — as little as $200–$400 per day toward an inpatient hospital stay. Ontario cancelled that program. Today, for practical purposes, OHIP covers nothing when you're outside Canada.

To put the exposure in perspective, here's what emergencies abroad can actually cost an uninsured Ontarian:

Emergency abroadTypical out-of-pocket cost

OHIP would cover $0 of those bills. Travel medical insurance is designed to cover almost all of it.

"But I have credit-card travel insurance"

Many premium credit cards include some travel medical coverage — and for short trips by younger, healthy cardholders it can be genuinely useful. But read the fine print before you rely on it:

  • Age limits — coverage often drops sharply or ends at age 60 or 65.
  • Trip-length caps — many cards cover only the first 8, 15, or 21 days of a trip.
  • Coverage limits — some cap out well below the cost of a serious hospitalization.
  • Pre-existing condition rules — usually stricter and easy to trip over.

Credit-card coverage is a supplement, not a substitute. If you're older, travelling longer, or managing any health condition, buy a dedicated policy.

Snowbird note: If you spend the winter down south, a standard annual multi-trip plan may only cover trips of up to 30 days. Snowbirds usually need a long-stay plan that extends a single trip to 90, 120, or 180+ days. Buying the wrong plan can leave you uninsured for the second half of your stay. What to buy instead of relying on OHIP

The fix is straightforward: an emergency travel medical policy purchased before you leave Canada. Key decisions are your coverage amount (commonly $1M+ in emergency medical), your deductible, and single-trip vs. annual multi-trip if you travel often. If you manage a health condition, make sure it's covered under the policy's "stability" rules — this is where most denied claims come from, and where an advisor earns their keep.

The bottom line

OHIP is designed for care inside Ontario. The moment you cross into another province you lose ambulance and drug coverage, and the moment you leave Canada you're effectively on your own. A few minutes and a modest premium replace a potential five- or six-figure bill. Don't leave it to chance.

Frequently asked questions

Does OHIP cover medical costs outside Canada?

Barely. Ontario ended the Out-of-Country Travellers Program in 2020, so OHIP no longer reimburses out-of-country emergency hospital or physician costs for most travellers. Any remaining coverage is capped at Ontario rates and covers only a tiny fraction of foreign medical bills.

Do I still need travel insurance if I have OHIP?

Yes. OHIP does not meaningfully cover emergency medical care abroad, so a private travel medical policy is essential whenever you leave the province — and especially when you leave Canada.

Does OHIP cover me in other Canadian provinces?

Mostly. OHIP covers medically necessary physician and hospital services in other provinces and territories (with a partial Quebec exception), but not ambulance fees or prescription drugs.

How much does travel insurance cost for Ontarians?

A single-trip emergency medical policy commonly ranges from about $15 for a young traveller on a short trip to several hundred dollars for older travellers on longer trips, depending on age, trip length, destination, and deductible.
