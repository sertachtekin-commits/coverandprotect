Super Visa Insurance Requirements in 2026: The Complete Checklist

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer: to meet IRCC's 2026 rules, Super Visa medical insurance must provide at least $100,000 in emergency coverage, stay valid for a full year from the date of entry, cover healthcare, hospitalization, and repatriation, and come from an approved insurer — with proof of paid coverage included in the application.

The insurance requirement is the piece of the Super Visa application that trips up the most families. Get it wrong and the whole application can be delayed or refused. This guide lays out exactly what IRCC requires in 2026, the documents you'll need, and the common mistakes to avoid.

The Super Visa insurance requirements (2026)

Every applicant must hold a qualifying medical insurance policy that meets all of the following:

  • Minimum $100,000 in coverage — the policy must provide at least $100,000 of emergency medical coverage. Some families choose higher limits for extra peace of mind, but $100,000 is the minimum that satisfies the requirement.
  • Valid for at least 1 year from entry — coverage must run for a minimum of 365 days starting from the applicant's arrival date in Canada.
  • Covers the essentials — the policy must cover healthcare, hospitalization, and repatriation (the cost of returning the person to their home country if needed).
  • From an approved insurer — the coverage must be from a Canadian insurer, or from an insurer outside Canada that IRCC has designated for the Super Visa program.
  • Proof of paid coverage — you must include evidence that the policy is paid and in force (or approved) with the visa application. A quote alone is not enough.
The rule that changed: Super Visa insurance used to be required from a Canadian insurer only. IRCC now also accepts coverage from designated insurers outside Canada. In practice, most families still choose a Canadian insurer — claims and support are far simpler to handle from within Canada if something goes wrong during the stay.

Documents you'll need for the application

The insurance policy is one part of a larger application. Alongside your qualifying coverage, IRCC expects:

Super Visa document checklist

  • Proof of Super Visa medical insurance — showing the $100,000 minimum, one-year validity, and that it's paid/approved.
  • Letter of invitation from your child or grandchild in Canada, promising financial support and describing the household.
  • Proof the host meets the minimum income (LICO) — the Canadian child or grandchild must show income at or above the Low Income Cut-Off for their family size (for example, a notice of assessment or employment letter).
  • Proof of relationship — documents showing the applicant is the parent or grandparent of the host.
  • Proof the host is a Canadian citizen or permanent resident.

The minimum income (LICO) threshold rises with the host's family size, and IRCC updates the figures each year — so confirm the current number for your household size before applying. Your Canadian child or grandchild is responsible for that part; the insurance is the piece we handle for you.

How long is the coverage — and the visa?

A Super Visa lets parents and grandparents stay up to five years per visit, and the visa itself can be valid for up to 10 years. Your insurance only needs to be valid for one year at a time, so many families buy a one-year policy for the application and then renew annually for as long as the parent or grandparent stays in Canada. If plans change, refund rules generally allow a refund if the visa is refused or the person leaves early.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Buying too little coverage — a policy under $100,000, or one that isn't valid for a full year, won't meet the requirement.
  • Submitting a quote instead of proof of purchase — IRCC wants evidence the policy is paid and in force (or approved), not just a price estimate.
  • Ignoring pre-existing conditions — if a parent manages a condition like diabetes or heart disease, the policy should cover stable pre-existing conditions, or a related claim could be denied.
  • Leaving it to the last minute — align the policy's start date with the expected arrival date, and give yourself time to get proof of coverage before the application deadline.
