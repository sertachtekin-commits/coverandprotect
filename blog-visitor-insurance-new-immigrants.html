Best Visitor Insurance for New Immigrants & Parents Coming to Canada (2026)

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Whether you've just landed as a permanent resident or you're bringing your parents to Canada for a long visit, there's a coverage gap most newcomers don't see coming: provincial health insurance often doesn't start on day one, and it never covers visitors at all. Private visitor to Canada insurance is what protects your family in the meantime.

This guide explains who needs coverage, the OHIP waiting period that catches new immigrants off guard, what to look for in a policy, and roughly what it costs in 2026.

The gap: provincial health coverage doesn't start immediately

In several provinces — Ontario among them — new permanent residents must wait before provincial health coverage (OHIP) begins. That waiting period can be up to three months from the date you establish residency. During those weeks, a single emergency — a fall, an infection, an accident — is entirely your financial responsibility unless you have private coverage.

This is exactly what newcomer / super visa / visitor insurance is built for: it bridges the gap until your provincial plan kicks in.

Action step for new PRs: Apply for your provincial health card the day you arrive so the waiting-period clock starts as early as possible — then buy private coverage to protect the months in between. The two work together.

Who needs visitor to Canada insurance?

  • New permanent residents during the provincial health waiting period.
  • Parents and grandparents visiting on a visitor visa or eTA.
  • Parents on a Super Visa — coverage is mandatory: at least $100,000, valid for 365 days.
  • Relatives, tourists, and returning workers not yet covered by a provincial plan.
  • Foreign workers and students in the gap before employer or institutional coverage begins.

What to look for in a good visitor policy

1. Enough emergency medical coverage

$100,000 is the Super Visa minimum and a sensible floor for anyone. Some families choose $150,000 or more for older visitors, given how quickly hospital costs add up.

2. Pre-existing condition coverage (done right)

If a parent manages diabetes, high blood pressure, or heart disease, look for a plan that covers stable pre-existing conditions and understand its "stability period" (commonly 90 or 180 days of no changes to medication or symptoms). Getting this wrong is the number-one reason claims are denied.

3. A deductible that fits your budget

Choosing a higher deductible lowers the premium. A $0 deductible costs more; a modest deductible you could comfortably pay in an emergency saves money.

4. Refund and change flexibility

Plans differ on refunds if the visitor leaves early or their visa is refused. Ask about refund terms before you buy — an advisor will flag the ones that matter.

Newcomer vs. Super Visa vs. tourist coverage — what's the difference?

They're closely related products, usually from the same insurers:

  • Visitor / tourist insurance — flexible emergency medical for any visitor; you choose the coverage and dates.
  • Super Visa insurance — a visitor policy that meets IRCC's specific rules ($100,000+, 365 days, Canadian insurer). Required to get the visa.
  • Newcomer insurance — visitor-style coverage sold to new PRs to bridge the provincial waiting period.

The right one depends on your family's immigration status and timeline — which is exactly the kind of thing worth a quick conversation before you buy.

Frequently asked questions

Do new immigrants to Canada need visitor insurance?

Often yes. In provinces like Ontario, new permanent residents face a waiting period (up to three months) before provincial health coverage begins. Private newcomer or visitor insurance covers emergencies during that gap.

Can I buy visitor insurance for my parents visiting Canada?

Yes. Visitor to Canada insurance is designed for parents, relatives, and tourists and covers emergency medical care during their stay. For parents on a Super Visa, the policy must provide at least $100,000 in coverage valid for 365 days.

Does visitor insurance cover pre-existing conditions?

Many plans offer optional coverage for stable pre-existing conditions, subject to a stability period (commonly 90 or 180 days). Coverage and definitions vary by insurer, so match the plan to the person's health.

How much does visitor insurance for Canada cost?

It depends mainly on age, coverage amount, and length of stay — roughly a few dollars per day for younger visitors up to $12–$20+ per day for the oldest visitors on higher-coverage plans.
