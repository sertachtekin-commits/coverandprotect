Travel Insurance for Canadians Going Abroad: 2026 Guide

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated June 2026 · 7 min read

If you're a Canadian heading abroad in 2026 — a week in the Caribbean, a month in Europe, or a whole winter down south — your provincial health plan covers almost none of your medical costs the moment you leave the country. Travel medical insurance is what stands between a vacation and a five-figure hospital bill.

Most travellers assume OHIP (or their province's equivalent) will reimburse them if something goes wrong overseas. It won't — not in any meaningful way. This guide explains exactly what travel insurance covers, the difference between single-trip and annual plans, what it costs in 2026, and how to pick the right policy for your trip.

Why your provincial health plan isn't enough

Provincial plans like OHIP were built for care inside your home province. When you travel outside Canada, reimbursement is capped at the rate the province would have paid at home — often just a few hundred dollars per day for a hospital stay that can actually cost $3,000–$10,000+ per day in places like the United States.

A few real-world examples of what travellers have faced out of pocket without coverage:

  • An emergency appendectomy in the U.S.: $30,000–$50,000+
  • A few days in a foreign ICU after a cardiac event: $100,000+
  • Air ambulance / medical repatriation back to Canada: $25,000–$200,000+

Your provincial plan might cover a tiny fraction of those amounts. Travel medical insurance is designed to cover the rest.

What travel medical insurance actually covers

A standard emergency travel medical policy for Canadians abroad typically includes:

  • Emergency hospital and medical care — doctors, hospital stays, surgery, diagnostics
  • Prescription drugs needed during an emergency
  • Emergency dental (usually for accidental injury, up to a limit)
  • Ambulance and medical transport, including air ambulance
  • Repatriation — getting you back to Canada for treatment, or returning remains
  • 24/7 emergency assistance hotline to coordinate care and guarantee payment to hospitals

Many travellers also add trip cancellation and interruption, lost or delayed baggage, and flight delay coverage. These protect your money rather than your health, and are often bundled as an all-inclusive package.

Single-trip vs. annual multi-trip: which do you need?

This is the most important cost decision for frequent travellers.

Single-trip insurance

Covers one specific trip from departure to return. Best if you travel abroad once or twice a year. You set the exact dates and destination, and pay only for those days.

Annual multi-trip insurance

One policy covers unlimited trips over 12 months, each up to a set maximum length (commonly 15, 30, or 60 days per trip). If you take three or more trips a year — or you're a business traveller — an annual plan is usually cheaper and far more convenient than buying coverage each time.

Snowbird tip: If you spend the winter in Florida, Arizona, or Mexico, watch the "maximum days per trip" limit on annual plans. A standard annual policy may only cover trips of up to 30 days. Snowbirds typically need a dedicated long-stay or "top-up" plan that extends a single trip to 90, 120, or 180+ days. Buying the wrong plan can leave you uninsured for the back half of your stay.

Pre-existing conditions and the "stability" rule

This is where most denied claims come from. If you manage a condition like high blood pressure, diabetes, or heart disease, your policy will only cover it if the condition has been stable for a defined period before departure (commonly 90 or 180 days) — meaning no changes to medication, dosage, treatment, or symptoms.

Answer the medical questionnaire honestly and carefully. A small inaccuracy can void your entire policy at claim time. If you're unsure how your medications affect eligibility, that's exactly the kind of thing an advisor sorts out before you buy.

6 mistakes Canadian travellers make

  • Assuming OHIP covers them abroad — it covers a tiny fraction at best.
  • Relying on credit-card travel insurance without reading the limits — coverage is often capped, age-restricted, or shorter than your trip.
  • Buying after they've left Canada — most policies must be purchased before departure.
  • Ignoring the stability period on pre-existing conditions and getting a claim denied.
  • Choosing a $0 deductible by default — a modest deductible can noticeably lower the premium.
  • Underestimating trip length on an annual plan and being uninsured past the per-trip day limit.

The bottom line

For Canadians, travel medical insurance isn't a luxury — it's the difference between a manageable emergency and a financial disaster. Single-trip plans suit occasional travellers; annual multi-trip plans reward anyone who travels a few times a year; and snowbirds need a long-stay plan built for their schedule.
