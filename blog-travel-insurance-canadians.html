Travel Insurance 2026 for Canadians: Costs, Coverage and How to Choose

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated July 15, 2026 · 9 min read

Travel insurance in 2026 should protect Canadians for the full length of the trip and include emergency medical care, medical evacuation and repatriation. The Government of Canada advises travellers to buy travel health and trip interruption insurance before leaving Canada — even for a one-day trip to the United States.

Travel insurance 2026: quick answer
  • Provincial health plans may cover none or only a small portion of medical expenses abroad.
  • Single-trip plans suit occasional travel; annual multi-trip plans can suit frequent travellers.
  • Snowbirds and long-stay travellers must check the maximum days allowed per trip.
  • Pre-existing conditions are governed by the policy's definition and stability period.
  • The cheapest policy is not necessarily the best policy if limits or exclusions do not match the trip.

This guide explains travel medical insurance outside Canada, typical 2026 planning costs, single-trip versus annual coverage, long-term travel insurance, pre-existing condition rules and the questions to ask before buying.

Why Canadians need travel insurance outside Canada

The Government of Canada warns that your provincial or territorial health plan may pay none or only a small part of medical costs abroad and will not pay foreign hospital bills up front. Hospitals and clinics may require immediate payment, and the Canadian government does not pay a traveller's medical bills.

That creates a substantial gap between provincial coverage and the actual cost of emergency treatment, hospitalization or medical evacuation. Private emergency travel medical insurance is designed to address eligible expenses within the policy's limits, terms and exclusions.

Official guidance: Government of Canada — trip interruption and travel health insurance.

What emergency travel medical insurance typically covers

Coverage varies by insurer and policy, but an emergency travel medical plan for Canadians abroad commonly includes eligible expenses for:

  • Emergency hospital and physician care — hospital stays, medical assessments, surgery and diagnostics
  • Emergency prescription medication related to a covered medical event
  • Emergency dental treatment, usually subject to a separate limit
  • Ground or air ambulance when medically necessary and approved
  • Medical evacuation to Canada or an appropriate treatment facility
  • Repatriation, including eligible arrangements following a covered death abroad
  • 24/7 emergency assistance to coordinate treatment and claims procedures

Trip cancellation, trip interruption, baggage loss, baggage delay and flight-delay benefits are separate from emergency medical coverage, although insurers may package them together in an all-inclusive plan.

When a single-trip plan makes sense

A single-trip policy covers one defined journey from the departure date to the return date. It is usually the more direct option for people who travel once or twice a year or who need a long uninterrupted period of coverage.

When an annual multi-trip plan makes sense

An annual plan covers multiple eligible trips during a 12-month period. It can reduce repeated applications and may be more economical for frequent travellers, but every trip must stay within the policy's maximum trip-duration limit.

Snowbird and long-term travel warning: A 30-day annual multi-trip plan does not automatically cover a 120-day winter stay. Snowbirds may need a dedicated long-stay policy or an approved top-up covering the additional days. Confirm how the policies coordinate before departure.

Travel insurance premiums are risk-based. The main pricing factors are:

  • Traveller age
  • Trip length and destination
  • Emergency-medical-only versus all-inclusive coverage
  • Coverage maximum and deductible
  • Medical history and pre-existing condition eligibility
  • Single-trip, annual multi-trip or long-stay structure

Pre-existing conditions and the stability period

A pre-existing condition is not automatically covered or excluded. The answer depends on the insurer's wording, the traveller's age and the required stability period.

Stability generally means that, during a specified period before departure, the medical condition and related treatment have met the policy's requirements. Depending on the policy, a medication change, new symptom, investigation, treatment change or medical consultation may affect stability.

Do not rely on assumptions. Answer every medical question accurately and ask for written clarification when a medication change, pending test or recent treatment may affect eligibility.

What travel insurance may not cover

Exclusions differ by policy. Common limitations may involve:

  • Routine checkups, elective procedures or non-emergency treatment
  • Unstable or excluded pre-existing conditions
  • High-risk sports or activities unless specifically covered
  • Alcohol- or drug-related incidents, depending on the wording
  • Travel to a destination subject to certain Government of Canada advisories
  • Claims made without following the emergency-assistance or pre-authorization process
  • Trips extending beyond the insured return date or maximum days per trip

Read the certificate and policy wording—not only the sales summary—before relying on coverage.

How to choose the best travel insurance for Canadians in 2026

There is no single best policy for every traveller. A suitable policy should match the trip and be understandable before purchase. Compare these items:

  1. Trip dates: Ensure coverage begins before departure and continues through the full return date.
  2. Emergency medical maximum: Confirm that the limit is appropriate for the destination and potential evacuation costs.
  3. Pre-existing condition wording: Review the definition, stability period and medical questionnaire.
  4. Deductible: A higher deductible may lower the premium but increases the amount paid during a claim.
  5. Emergency assistance: Confirm 24/7 contact availability and the required claims process.
  6. Activities and destinations: Check exclusions for sports, cruises, remote travel and government advisories.
  7. Cancellation protection: Add trip cancellation or interruption coverage when prepaid, non-refundable costs are significant.
  8. Existing coverage: Review employer, retiree and credit-card insurance for age limits, trip-length limits and exclusions before assuming it is sufficient.

Common travel insurance mistakes

  • Assuming OHIP or another provincial plan is enough for treatment outside Canada
  • Buying based only on the lowest premium without comparing limits and exclusions
  • Relying on credit-card coverage without checking eligibility, age and trip-duration restrictions
  • Ignoring a medication or health change that may affect the stability requirement
  • Using an annual plan beyond its per-trip day limit
  • Failing to contact the emergency-assistance centre when the policy requires prompt notification

Travel insurance FAQ

What is the best travel insurance for Canadians in 2026?

The best policy is the one that covers the complete trip, fits the traveller's medical situation and destination, includes appropriate emergency medical and evacuation benefits, and has terms the traveller understands. Compare coverage—not only price.

Does OHIP cover medical care outside Canada?

Provincial coverage may pay none or only a small portion of eligible expenses abroad and does not normally pay foreign providers up front. It should not be treated as a replacement for private travel medical insurance.

How much does travel insurance cost in Canada in 2026?

Pricing varies by age, trip length, destination, coverage type, deductible, medical history and insurer. Short trips for younger travellers may cost tens of dollars; long trips, older ages, U.S. travel and all-inclusive plans can cost substantially more.

Is annual multi-trip insurance cheaper?

It can be more cost-effective for frequent travellers, but the policy still limits the duration of each trip. Compare annual premiums against the cost of the individual trips you expect to take.

Can I buy travel insurance after leaving Canada?

Most standard coverage should be purchased before departure. Specialized after-departure products may exist, but they can include waiting periods and narrower options.
