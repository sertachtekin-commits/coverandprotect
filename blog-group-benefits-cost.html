How Much Do Group Benefits Cost for a Small Business in Ontario? (2026 Guide)

By Sertac Tekin, Licensed Ontario Insurance Advisor (FSRA #10112782) · Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer: for most small Ontario businesses in 2026, group benefits cost roughly $75 to $250 per employee per month — about $900 to $3,000 per employee per year. Where your plan lands depends on your team's size, ages, industry, and how rich you make the coverage.

If you're an owner weighing a benefits plan for the first time — whether you run a shop, an office, a trades crew, or a growing startup — the cost question is usually the first hurdle. This guide breaks down real 2026 pricing by plan tier, what pushes the price up or down, how the tax treatment works in your favour, and the legitimate ways to keep it affordable.

2026 group benefits cost by plan tier

Group benefits are modular — you choose which coverages to include, so the price scales with how comprehensive the plan is. Here's a general guide to monthly cost per employee for a small Ontario business. These are illustrative ranges to help you budget; your actual quote depends on your specific group.

Basic

$75–$130/employee/mo
  • Extended health (drugs, paramedical)
  • Basic dental
  • Vision (often optional)

Standard

$130–$190/employee/mo
  • Everything in Basic
  • Group life & AD&D
  • Short- or long-term disability

Comprehensive

$190–$250+/employee/mo
  • Everything in Standard
  • Enhanced dental & paramedical caps
  • EAP, critical illness, or a Health Spending Account

A practical way to budget: multiply your headcount by your target per-employee cost. A 5-person team on a Standard plan at ~$160/employee is roughly $800/month, or about $9,600/year — before the tax deduction (more on that below).

What makes the price go up or down

1. Group size

The number of employees matters. Very small groups (2–4 people) are priced more cautiously because a single big claim has a larger impact on the pool. As your team grows, pricing tends to stabilize and per-employee cost often improves.

2. Employee demographics

Average age and the mix of single vs. family coverage are major drivers. A younger team costs less to insure than an older one, and employees who elect family coverage (spouse and children) cost more than single members.

3. Industry and occupation

Your line of work affects disability and life pricing in particular. Physically demanding trades — construction, manufacturing, transport — typically carry higher disability rates than desk-based office work, because the risk of an injury that keeps someone off the job is higher.

4. Plan design

This is the lever you control most. Deductibles, co-insurance (say the plan pays 80% and the employee 20%), annual maximums on paramedical and dental, and drug plan design all move the premium. A leaner design meaningfully lowers cost; a richer one raises it.

5. Claims history

Once a plan has been in force, its own claims experience feeds into renewal pricing. A year of high claims can push next year's premium up; a stable, healthy group helps keep renewals in check.

The tax advantage: Premiums your business pays for a group plan are generally a deductible business expense, and for employees, employer-paid health and dental benefits are normally received tax-free (outside Quebec). That means a benefits dollar often goes further than the same dollar paid as taxable salary — a key reason benefits are such an effective retention tool. Confirm the specifics with your advisor or accountant.

5 ways to keep a small-business plan affordable

  • Share the premium — splitting cost with employees (commonly 50/50 to 80/20 via payroll) is the most direct way to fit the plan to your budget while still offering real value.
  • Start lean, then grow — launch with a Basic or Standard plan and add modules (disability, enhanced dental, critical illness) as the business grows and cash flow allows.
  • Use co-insurance and sensible caps — an 80% co-insurance level and reasonable annual maximums on paramedical/dental trim premium without gutting the benefit.
  • Add a Health Spending Account (HSA) — an HSA gives employees a fixed, predictable pool of tax-effective dollars, capping your cost while offering flexibility. It pairs well with a lean base plan.
  • Work with an independent advisor — an advisor markets your group to multiple insurers, compares the quotes apples-to-apples, and structures the plan design so you're not overpaying for coverage your team won't use.

Is a group plan worth it for a small business?

For most owners, yes — for two reasons beyond the coverage itself. First, benefits are one of the strongest tools for attracting and keeping good employees, especially in competitive trades and skilled roles where wages alone aren't enough. Second, the tax treatment makes benefits a more efficient form of compensation than an equivalent raise. If you have even two or three employees you want to retain, a modest plan is usually worth pricing out.

A group plan also complements individual protection your key people may already need — for example, critical illness coverage that pays a tax-free lump sum on a serious diagnosis, which many owners add for themselves and key staff.

The bottom line

Small-business group benefits in Ontario typically cost $75–$250 per employee per month, driven by your group's size, ages, industry, and plan design — and softened by a business tax deduction and tax-free health benefits for staff.
